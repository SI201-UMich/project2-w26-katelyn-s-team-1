In this project, I realized that the accountability system for short term rentals in San Francisco isn't very strong. 
Since the city only checks listings after someone complains,a lot of missing policy numbers can go unnoticed. 
After scraping the data, I saw how easy it is for hosts to enter anything they want. I think a better system would require 
Airbnbto verify policy nums before a listing goes live. That would shift the responsibility to the platform. This also 
fits with the idea in the SWE Code of Ethics about making systems more trustworthy.

While working on this project, I also learned about the legality of web scraping in US and how it's unclear. One factor 
is whether the data is truly public and accessed without bypassinganything. Some court cases say scraping public pages 
is allowed, while others say it can violate the Computer Fraud and Abuse act. This matters because scraping can be useful 
but it can also cross legal lines without people realizing.

Finally, scraping public data isn't ethical all the time. An example like Clearciew AI shows how data can be used in ways 
people never expected. Two guidelines I think are important are: 1: only scrape data when the purpose is responsible and 
benefits people. 2: consider whether people would reasonably expect their data to be collected and analyzed. This project 
helped me see both the technical and ethical sides of scraping.
